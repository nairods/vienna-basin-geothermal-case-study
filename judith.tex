\begin{abstract}
The reliable operation of an electric power system depends on its ability to remain in, or return to, a steady operating state after disturbances. Decarbonisation displaces synchronous machines with inverter-based renewable resources, reducing rotational inertia and tightening the time scales over which the system must be balanced and protected. This paper first summarises the classical and extended definitions of power-system stability and the principal challenge posed by low-inertia operation, then reviews how artificial intelligence (AI) and machine-learning (ML) techniques can support stability during the energy transition: forecasting, state estimation and anomaly detection, real-time control, and predictive maintenance. The limitations and risks of deploying AI in safety-critical grid operation are also discussed.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Power-system stability is the property of an electric power system that enables it to remain in a state of operating equilibrium under normal conditions and to regain an acceptable equilibrium after a disturbance~\cite{kundur1994}. Maintaining stability is a precondition for reliable electricity supply; instability can cascade into widespread blackouts within seconds. As power systems decarbonise, the physical characteristics of generation are changing rapidly, and with them the nature of the stability problem~\cite{hatziargyriou2021revisited}. Inverter-based renewable resources (IBRs) such as photovoltaic plants and wind farms do not, by default, provide the rotational inertia that synchronous machines do; the rate of change of frequency (RoCoF) after a contingency rises, narrowing the window in which protection and control systems must act~\cite{milano2018foundations}. At the same time, variability of renewable output and the rapid growth of flexible loads (electric vehicles, heat pumps) demand operational decisions that are faster, more granular, and more numerous than human operators or classical optimisation tools were designed for. This combination of tighter physical margins and rising operational complexity is what motivates the application of AI to the modern grid.

\section{Background: Stability under Decarbonisation}
The IEEE/CIGRE joint task force on stability terms defined power-system stability through rotor-angle, frequency, and voltage stability, each subdivided by time scale and disturbance magnitude~\cite{kundur2004definition}. In 2021 the taxonomy was extended to include converter-driven stability and resonance stability, reflecting the dominance of fast power-electronic dynamics in modern grids~\cite{hatziargyriou2021revisited}. Maintaining the ``essential reliability services'' on which stability depends---frequency response, ramping capability, voltage support---requires explicit planning as the resource mix changes~\cite{nerc2015erstf}, and is being stressed by an increasing frequency of extreme weather events such as the 2021 Texas cold-weather outages~\cite{fercnerc2021texas}. Hardware mitigations such as grid-forming inverters, synchronous condensers, fast battery storage and HVDC interconnection address the physical side of the problem~\cite{lasseter2020gridforming,entsoe2020inertia}; AI complements this by improving how the existing assets are forecast, monitored, and controlled.

\section{AI Applications for Grid Stability}
Recent surveys identify four broad domains in which AI is being applied to power-system operation: forecasting, monitoring, control, and asset management~\cite{duchesne2020ml,ahmad2021ai}.

\subsection{Forecasting of Generation, Demand and Flexibility}
Accurate short-term forecasts of renewable generation and demand are the most direct way to convert uncertainty into manageable scheduling. Deep-learning architectures---recurrent, convolutional and graph-based---consistently outperform classical statistical methods on intra-day and day-ahead horizons, particularly for wind and photovoltaic output~\cite{ahmad2021ai}. Better forecasts reduce the operating reserve required to cover renewable variability, which in turn allows operators to commit less synchronous backup generation while still meeting reliability standards. Forecasting is also being extended to flexible demand: reinforcement-learning agents have been used to dispatch demand response and behind-the-meter storage in ways that smooth net load~\cite{vazquez2019rl}.

\subsection{State Estimation and Anomaly Detection}
Modern transmission systems are instrumented with phasor measurement units (PMUs) producing high-rate synchronised samples that classical state estimators were not designed to exploit fully. ML models can fuse PMU streams with traditional SCADA measurements to estimate system state, identify bad data, and flag cyber-physical anomalies in time scales suitable for real-time use~\cite{duchesne2020ml}. Graph neural networks have been used to perform fast contingency screening, identifying which lines or nodes are most at risk under a proposed dispatch and allowing operators to prioritise mitigations~\cite{donnot2018contingencies}.

\subsection{Real-Time and Topology Control}
Optimal-power-flow and security-constrained dispatch problems become combinatorially harder as the share of inverter-based resources grows and as operators are asked to consider more contingencies. Reinforcement learning (RL) is being investigated as a tool for high-dimensional, time-critical control where classical formulations are too slow~\cite{glavic2017rl}. The L2RPN (``Learning to Run a Power Network'') challenges have shown that RL agents can learn topology-control policies that keep simulated grids within operational limits under contingencies, opening a path toward learned assistants for control-room operators~\cite{marot2020l2rpn}. ML is also being embedded inside inverter controllers to synthesise grid-forming behaviour and to adapt protection settings as inertia and short-circuit levels change with the resource mix.

\subsection{Predictive Maintenance and Asset Management}
Equipment failure on transmission and distribution networks is a common precipitating cause of cascading instability. ML applied to vibration sensors, oil chromatography, partial-discharge waveforms and weather data can predict transformer, switchgear and line-component failures days to weeks in advance, reducing both unplanned outages and the conservative maintenance margins that previously absorbed this risk~\cite{ahmad2021ai}.

\section{Limitations and Risks}
The case for AI in grid operation comes with substantial caveats. ML models are only as good as their training distribution, and renewable assets, load patterns and topology change faster than typical training data are refreshed---raising the risk of \emph{distribution shift} in safety-critical operation. Most modern ML methods are also difficult to interpret, which conflicts with regulatory expectations of explainable, auditable operational decisions; work on interpretable surrogate models is one response~\cite{cremer2019interpretable}. ML pipelines introduce new cyber-physical attack surfaces, and their failure modes can be harder to characterise than those of the classical algorithms they replace. The International Energy Agency has stressed that the energy and digital sectors must co-evolve, and that AI deployment in critical infrastructure requires deliberate governance rather than incidental adoption~\cite{iea2023ai}.

\section{Conclusion}
Decarbonisation is making the grid harder to stabilise: less inertia, more variability, more devices, and tighter control time scales. AI does not replace the physical mitigations---grid-forming inverters, storage, synchronous condensers, stronger interconnection---but it can extract substantially more reliability from existing assets through better forecasting, faster state estimation, smarter dispatch, and earlier fault detection. Whether AI delivers on this promise in practice will depend less on algorithmic progress than on data quality, model governance, and the careful integration of learned components into a stack that has historically prized transparency and predictability.
