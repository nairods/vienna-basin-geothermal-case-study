\section*{Geothermal Heat for Vienna: Seven Research Threads}

\subsection*{Why this scope}

Our preceding analysis converged on geothermal \emph{heat} (not electricity)
for Vienna's district-heating network as the most defensible target for a
physics retreat investigation. At Vienna Basin reservoir temperatures
($\approx 85$--$150\,^\circ$C), the exergy argument strongly favours direct
heat use, and Vienna's $\approx 6$~TWh/yr district-heat demand can in principle
be covered by $\sim 40$ doublets of the kind already being drilled in
Aspern--Donaustadt.

The complication is that this top-level story is already being executed by
Wien Energie and OMV through their joint venture \texttt{deeep Tiefengeothermie
GmbH} (established 2023): three exploratory wells were drilled by mid-2025 at
the Aspern site, surface facilities are being built from 2026, and a $20$~MW$_{\mathrm{th}}$
plant is scheduled to come online in 2028, with up to $\sim 120$~MW$_{\mathrm{th}}$
of capacity planned across Donaustadt and Simmering by 2030 [1, 2]. Simply
reproducing this engineering case study is therefore an exercise, not research.

The seven threads below were chosen specifically because each addresses a
question the utility roadmap does \emph{not} fully cover, while remaining
tractable within a retreat-then-followup timescale. Threads 1--4 are the
original set drafted earlier; threads 5--7 were added in a second pass and
deliberately reach into adjacent physics (systems-level comparison,
working-fluid thermodynamics, multi-decade reservoir behaviour).

\paragraph{F1 --- Vienna Basin geology and reservoir-target stratigraphy.}
The Vienna Basin is a Miocene thin-skinned pull-apart at the junction of
the Eastern Alps, Western Carpathians, and Pannonian Basin system,
formed by $\sim 30$~km sinistral displacement on the Vienna Basin
Transform Fault System (VBTFS) at a Miocene-averaged slip rate of
$\sim 4$~mm/a; subsidence initiated $\sim 15.5$~Ma (Badenian) and
terminated $\sim 7$--$8$~Ma [33, 34]. The geothermal-relevant
stratigraphy targets two horizons:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lllll}
\hline
\textbf{Target horizon} & \textbf{Age} & \textbf{Depth~[m]} & \textbf{Approx.\ $T$~[$^\circ$C]} & \textbf{Conf.} \\
\hline
Badenian sandstones    & Mid Miocene & 1000--2000 & 40--75   & A \\
Aderklaa Conglomerate  & L.\ Miocene & 2000--3500 & 70--110  & A \\
(Rothneusiedl Fm)      &             &            &          &   \\
Hauptdolomit basement  & M.--U.\ Triassic & 3000--5000 & 100--150 & B \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Reported Vienna Basin geothermal gradients cluster around
$22$--$33\,^\circ$C/km in the basin centre, with values up to
$30$--$50\,^\circ$C/km locally; some peripheral wells show
\emph{lower} effective gradients owing to convective cooling by
descending fresh meteoric water (the Berndorf-1 anomaly,
$\sim 40$~km SW of Vienna, shows formation temperatures down to
6~km that are $\sim 100$~K cooler than expected from the regional
heat-flow [33]). For comparison, the neighbouring Pannonian Basin runs
$\sim 90$~mW/m$^2$ and $\sim 45\,^\circ$C/km, so Vienna Basin is the
``cooler relative'' of an otherwise hot region [conf.\ A]. The
Aderklaa-96 measurement (thread~1) of $\sim 102\,^\circ$C at
$\sim 2900$~m is consistent with the central-basin gradient.

Badenian sandstone porosity follows a standard compaction trend:
$\sim 30\%$ at 1000~m, falling to $\sim 15\%$ at 2700~m [35]. The
Aderklaa Conglomerate is interpreted as braided-river facies with
heterogeneous grain size and good but spatially variable
permeability [33, 34]. The Hauptdolomit basement is karstified
and fracture-controlled rather than matrix-controlled, with
strongly heterogeneous permeability that is the dominant
exploration risk for this target [conf.\ B].

\paragraph{F6 --- Austrian regulatory framework (Phase~3f addition).}
Deep geothermal in Austria is regulated as a mining activity under the
Federal Mining Act (\emph{Mineralrohstoffgesetz}, MinroG) [49];
reinjection is additionally governed by the Water Rights Act
(\emph{Wasserrechtsgesetz}, WRG) [50]. Projects above 20~MW$_{\mathrm{th}}$
or with the potential for ``significant environmental impact'' fall
under the EIA Act (\emph{Umweltvertr\"aglichkeitspr\"ufungsgesetz},
UVP-G 2000) [51] --- which includes the Wien~Energie Aspern plant at
20~MW$_{\mathrm{th}}$ Stage~1 and certainly the longer-term
multi-doublet build-out. Austrian EIA timelines run 18--48~months
typically; the ``2028 commissioning'' target for Aspern is therefore
on a tight schedule and assumes no Aarhus Convention objection
proceedings. Project finance for renewable heat in Austria can draw
on the Renewable Heat Act (\emph{Erneuerbare-W\"arme-Gesetz}, EWG)
subsidies, the Climate and Energy Fund (KLIEN) deep-geothermal
funding line, and EAG/OeMAG payments for renewable-heat coupling
where applicable. \emph{None of these are quantified in the
LCOH calculation above; their inclusion would lower the
geothermal effective cost by a further $\sim 5$--$15$~EUR/MWh in
favourable scenarios.} A full regulatory compliance + finance
mapping is a deferred Phase~4 task for whoever takes this case
study forward; the citations [49]--[51] are entry points.

\textbf{What the comparisons tell us.}
\begin{itemize}
\item Aspern reservoir temperatures ($\sim 100\,^\circ$C from the
Aderklaa Conglomerate at $\sim 2.9$~km) are \emph{lower} than the
Bavarian Molasse Malm (90--150~$^\circ$C at 2.5--4~km) but
\emph{higher} than Szeged (90--94~$^\circ$C) and well above the Paris
Basin Dogger (55--85~$^\circ$C). The Aspern conditions are mid-range
for European hydrothermal DH --- not unusually challenging.
\item Per-doublet thermal power (18.8~MW$_{\mathrm{th}}$ frozen for
Vienna) is well-aligned with Bavarian Molasse plant averages
($\sim 12$--$25$~MW per plant) and well above Dogger doublets
($\sim 5$--$10$~MW). Munich--Riem's single 13~MW$_{\mathrm{th}}$
doublet is the closest in scale to a Vienna~Basin doublet.
\item The full Bavarian Molasse fleet ($\sim 25$ plants over
$\sim 20$~years) is the natural operational analogue: same regulatory
regime, similar reservoir, and a substantial body of public data on
doublet success rate, scaling (Sauerlach BaSO$_4$), and induced
seismicity (Poing $M\sim 2.1$). Adopting Bavarian operational lessons
is the most direct de-risking path for Wien~Energie.
\item Szeged is the most relevant Pannonian-edge benchmark: lower-T
sandstone reservoir, replacing $\sim 20\cdot 10^{6}$~m$^3$/yr natural
gas (39.3~kt~CO$_2$/yr avoided). At the Vienna~Basin 2040 target
(4~TWh/yr from geothermal+HP), the avoided CO$_2$ would be
$\sim 800$~kt/yr at gas-CHP-equivalent intensity --- an order of
magnitude larger.
\item Reykjav\'ik is the aspirational benchmark: 100 years of
operation, 4~kW thermal per user. Vienna's long-term aspiration
($\sim 200{,}000$ households at $\sim 5$--$10$~kW each) is
geographically realistic by this ratio.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Thread 1 --- Repurposing OMV legacy oil and gas wells}

\paragraph{State of the art.}
Repurposing of legacy hydrocarbon wells for geothermal use has become a
distinct research subfield in the last five years. A 2025 review article
in \emph{Applied Energy} catalogues the four main technical concepts
[11]: (i) direct conversion to an open-loop production
well after re-completion; (ii) coaxial closed-loop single-well borehole heat
exchangers (BHE); (iii) CO$_2$-based thermosiphon designs descended from
Brown (2000); and (iv) use as observation/monitoring wells in a larger
field. Closed-loop coaxial BHEs are the most actively studied because they
sidestep the formation-water risk that complicates open-loop reuse.
Reported single-well thermal outputs are modest: $\sim 600$~kW for a
4~km coaxial BHE under optimal conditions in a numerical study
[12], up to $\sim 2$~MW$_\mathrm{th}$ at early times in the
Mol field case study in Belgium [13]. North American activity is
concentrated in jurisdictions with large orphaned-well inventories
(Alberta, Oklahoma, Colorado), with Gradient Geothermal among the first
commercial movers in the US.

\paragraph{Vienna Basin specifics.}
The Vienna Basin has hundreds of legacy hydrocarbon wells drilled by OMV
and its predecessor RAG since 1949, concentrated in the Matzen,
Sch\"onkirchen, Aderklaa and Bockflie{\ss} fields [5].
Crucially, OMV has \emph{already} initiated a Vienna-Basin pilot:
the Aderklaa-96 well, originally a $\sim 2900$~m gas well drilled in
the 1970s, was re-opened in 2022--2023 for geothermal testing
[3]. Production and injection testing yielded a bottom-hole
temperature of $\approx 102\,^\circ$C in the Hauptdolomit basement and a
reinjection rate up to $\sim 40$~m$^3$/h [4]. This is a live
case study a retreat can build around --- the well exists, the data are
partly public, the depth and temperatures are representative of a much
larger inventory.

\paragraph{Aderklaa-96 open-loop vs.\ coaxial closed-loop ---
quick Phase~2 estimate.} For the open-loop production
configuration (one well producing, one injecting --- the canonical
doublet) the published Aderklaa-96 test gives
$T_{\mathrm{prod}}\approx 102\,^\circ$C and reinjection
$\sim 40$~m$^3$/h~$\approx 11.1$~kg/s. With $\Delta T = 60$~K and
$c_p = 4180$~J/(kg\,K):
$P_{\mathrm{open}} \approx 11.1 \times 4180 \times 60 \approx
2.8$~MW$_{\mathrm{th}}$ at the measured test flow rate, scaling
linearly to $\sim 18.8$~MW$_{\mathrm{th}}$ at the Aspern target
flow rate of $\sim 75$~kg/s. This is the Phase~2-frozen reference
doublet output.

For a single-well coaxial closed-loop \emph{retrofit}
of Aderklaa-96 (no formation-water contact), comparable Mol
(Belgium) field data [13] and the Hu \emph{et al.}\ Hinton, Alberta
modelling [12] suggest steady-state extraction of $\sim 0.6$--$2$~MW
for a 2.9~km coaxial BHE at this temperature regime. The
practical loss factor versus open-loop is large
($\sim 10$--$20\times$) and is fundamental: a coaxial pipe has
limited contact area with the formation, whereas an open-loop
doublet exploits the entire aquifer volume. The retrofit case is
therefore not competitive on $\mathrm{MW}/\mathrm{well}$ terms;
its merit lies in eliminating formation-water risk (corrosion,
brine handling, induced-pressure effects) and in being feasible
for casing-constrained legacy wells where a doublet is not.

\textbf{Heuristic ranking for OMV-inventory repurposing.} Combining
the Phase~2 numbers with the cost asymmetry between drilling
(dominant) and surface plant (modest), the defensible heuristic
ordering of repurposing strategies is:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{Best:} legacy well as one half of a new open-loop
doublet (drill only the second well). Saves $\sim$EUR~10--15~M
per project --- material for the LCOH.
\item \emph{Niche:} coaxial closed-loop in casing-friendly,
high-temperature legacy wells in locations where land use blocks
a second well. Modest output, modest cost.
\item \emph{Marginal:} CO$_2$ thermosiphon variant
(see thread~6). Working-fluid corrosion and material questions
are still open.
\item \emph{Default:} plug-and-abandon, unchanged.
\end{enumerate}
The size of (1) depends on the geographic distribution and
condition of OMV's legacy well stock --- a database query we
cannot perform from public sources.

\paragraph{Scaling and corrosion in the reinjection loop
(Phase~3f addition).} Routine low-enthalpy geothermal operation
faces three well-documented chemistry hazards that the LCOH
calculation in T5 silently assumes away. (1) \emph{Carbonate
scaling}: at the producer wellhead, depressurisation drives
CO$_2$ exsolution and CaCO$_3$ precipitation; the Hauptdolomit
host rock (high-Ca brines, $T \sim 100\,^\circ$C, near-saturated
in calcite at reservoir conditions) is a classic scaling regime.
Bavarian Molasse Basin plants typically dose with scale
inhibitor and accept $\sim 5$--$10\%$ availability loss to
clean-out shutdowns. (2) \emph{Sulfate scaling (anhydrite,
barite)}: where deep brines contain Sr/Ba and shallow mixing
introduces SO$_4$, BaSO$_4$ deposits in producer tubing
are a chronic problem (Sauerlach 2014 case). (3) \emph{H$_2$S
corrosion} of carbon steel casings: produced waters from deep
Hauptdolomit often contain $\sim 10$--$100$~ppm H$_2$S, which
attacks carbon-steel tubulars (the dominant material in OMV
legacy wells). Standard practice is L80 SS or CRA tubing inside
the producer hot zone; cost premium $\sim 1.5$--$3\times$
carbon steel. \emph{Implication for LCOH}: a 5\% capacity-factor
loss to scaling-related downtime plus CRA tubing on producers
adds $\sim 2$--$5$~EUR/MWh to the headline 17~EUR/MWh
geothermal LCOH. This is at the order-of-magnitude of the
midpoint--high-range CapEx uncertainty; the geo:HP ratio
remains favourable but the absolute number tightens. A real
PHREEQC saturation-index analysis on Aderklaa-96 produced-water
chemistry is the right Phase~4 task (and is itself blocked by
the same OMV-chemistry external-access gap that T3 already
flags).

\paragraph{Open questions for the retreat.}
\begin{itemize}
\item For Aderklaa-96 specifically: what \emph{steady-state} thermal output
is achievable in coaxial closed-loop mode versus the measured open-loop
performance? Build a 1-D BHE heat-transfer model with the published
geometry and Hauptdolomit thermal properties.
\item Statistical question: given the OMV well inventory in the basin
(depth, temperature, casing condition), what fraction is plausibly
repurposable? Even rough screening criteria would be useful.
\item Wellbore-integrity physics: how does 40--60 years of cement aging,
casing corrosion, and possible $\mathrm{H_2S}$ exposure constrain
re-completion options at OMV-typical depths (1.5--3~km)?
\item CO$_2$ as working fluid: in the closed-loop coaxial geometry,
how much does the supercritical CO$_2$ thermosiphon effect change
thermal output versus water? Vienna Basin temperatures sit close to
the supercritical CO$_2$ regime --- the comparison is non-trivial.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Thread 2 --- Induced seismicity at Vienna Basin conditions}

\paragraph{Why this is not the same problem as Basel or Pohang.}
The Basel 2006 ($M_L\,3.4$) and Pohang 2017 ($M_w\,5.5$) cautionary cases
were Enhanced Geothermal System (EGS) projects in crystalline basement,
involving high-pressure stimulation that reactivated previously unknown
faults. A Vienna Basin hydrothermal project at $\sim 3$~km in sedimentary
host rock, with modest injection over-pressure, is a fundamentally
different operating regime. The relevant analog is the
\emph{Bavarian Molasse Basin} fleet of low-enthalpy hydrothermal plants
(Unterhaching, Pullach, Sauerlach, Geretsried, Poing) ---
geologically and operationally far closer to what Wien Energie plans.

\paragraph{What the Molasse Basin tells us.}
The Poing plant near Munich produced a sequence of $M \approx 2.1$ induced
events about five years after circulation began, with microseismicity
spatially correlated with the injection well
[9]. Numerical ground-motion simulations indicate that
even the largest of these events did not exceed the German
$\mathrm{PGV} = 5$~mm/s damage threshold. The Greater Munich
\emph{INSIDE} research project (BMWi-funded) has since taken on the
broader question of how to monitor and forecast seismicity in a basin
where multiple plants share the same fault system [10].
The headline result: felt induced events are possible from
hydrothermal-scale injection, but stay well below the EGS regime in
both magnitude and damage potential.

\paragraph{Vienna Basin specifics.}
The Vienna Basin is a Miocene pull-apart basin transected by the
left-lateral Vienna Basin Transfer Fault (VBTF). Historical seismicity
along the VBTF reaches $I_{\max,\mathrm{obs}}/M_{\max,\mathrm{obs}} = 8/5.2$,
with major events including the 4th-century AD Carnuntum earthquake, the
2000 Ebreichsdorf $M\,4.8$, the 2013 Ebreichsdorf series ($M\,4.3$),
and a 2024 April $M\approx 3$ event likely on a normal splay fault
of the VBTF [6, 7, 8].
The Hintersberger \& Decker (2018) palaeoseismological study of the
Markgrafneusiedl Fault is particularly relevant: it argues that splay
faults of the VBTF are active despite the \emph{absence} of historical
events on them, and that $M \geq 7.0$ earthquakes should be considered
in hazard assessment for the Vienna metropolitan area [6].
This is a strong reason to take the seismicity question seriously even
under "modest" hydrothermal operating conditions.

\paragraph{The Aderklaa Fault System (AFS) --- the local stress regime
(Levi \emph{et al.}\ 2024).} A direct Vienna-Basin-specific hazard study,
Levi \emph{et al.}\ (2024) [44], analyses borehole image data and a 1D
geomechanical stress model built on open-hole logs, leak-off tests, and
image logs from a $\sim 4220$~m deep Vienna~Basin geothermal exploration
well that targets fractured Triassic carbonates (Hauptdolomit) below the
Miocene fill. The well penetrates \emph{several normal faults of the
Aderklaa Fault System (AFS)}; these faults offset Pleistocene terraces
at the surface and are therefore regarded as tectonically active in the
Holocene. Stress-induced borehole failures and the 1D model jointly
constrain the stress regime to \emph{normal faulting},
$S_v > S_{H,\max} > S_{h,\min}$, at the targeted reservoir depths. The
combination of (i) active normal faults, (ii) a normal-fault stress regime
at depth, and (iii) a planned doublet immediately adjacent is the
specific configuration in which pore-pressure increases on the
production/injection cycle produce \emph{direct} Mohr--Coulomb
destabilisation.

\paragraph{Coulomb stress estimate for the Aspern doublet
(Phase~2 analysis).} For a normal-faulting regime with a critically
stressed fault, the Coulomb failure stress change from pore pressure
alone is, to leading order,
$\Delta\sigma_{\mathrm{Coulomb}} \approx \Delta p$ (effective-stress
direct contribution; shear-stress changes are second order for
modest injection pressures). Order-of-magnitude estimate for Aspern:
\begin{itemize}
\item Reinjection over-pressure at the injector wellhead: typically
$\sim 2$--$5$~MPa above hydrostatic for sedimentary-reservoir
hydrothermal doublets at $\sim 75$~kg/s.
\item Pressure diffusion radius after $t = 30$~yr operation, with
sedimentary aquifer hydraulic diffusivity $D \sim 0.1$--$1$~m$^2$/s:
$r \sim \sqrt{Dt} \approx 1.8$--$5.5$~km. The AFS splays lie
\emph{within} this radius for the Aspern site.
\item Pressure perturbation at $r = 2$~km (typical AFS fault distance):
$\Delta p(r) \sim 0.1$--$1$~MPa under steady-state injection.
\item Thermal-stress contribution from the cooled reservoir volume
(thread~7 result): an additional $\sim 0.1$--$1$~MPa at km-scale
distance from the doublet over 30~yr [32].
\end{itemize}
\emph{Combined Coulomb stress perturbation on AFS splay faults
under nominal Aspern operation: $\sim 0.2$--$2$~MPa over decadal
timescales.} (Decomposed: pore-pressure piece $\sim 0.1$--$1$~MPa;
thermo-elastic piece from the cooled volume $\sim 0.1$--$1$~MPa.
These two contributions are not independent --- they share the
same well and reservoir-geometry inputs --- and they are summed
here as an upper-bound combination, not as expected additive
effects.) For comparison, the critical-stress threshold for
triggering on an already-critically-loaded fault is, empirically,
$\sim 0.01$--$0.1$~MPa. The Aspern numbers exceed the
\emph{upper-bound} threshold (0.1~MPa) by a factor of $\sim 2$ in
the low case and $\sim 20$ in the high case --- i.e.\ between
$\sim 0.3$ and $\sim 1.3$ orders of magnitude. The combined
perturbation always sits above the lower-bound threshold
(0.01~MPa). The question is therefore not ``\emph{whether}
pressure perturbations reach critically stressed AFS faults'' but
``\emph{which} faults are critically stressed enough to respond,
and at what fraction of the perturbation envelope.'' The latter
is what Levi \emph{et al.}\ [44] are addressing.

\paragraph{Operational analogue: Bavarian Molasse Basin.} The
$\sim 20$ low-enthalpy hydrothermal plants in the Bavarian Molasse
Basin operate in a comparable geological setting (sedimentary
carbonates, normal-fault stress regime at the relevant depths,
hydrothermal --- not EGS --- operating conditions). Their
operational record over $\sim 15$~years includes:
\begin{itemize}
\item Microseismicity onset typically several years after
circulation begins (Poing: $\sim 5$~yr; cf.\ Keil \emph{et al.}\ [9]).
\item Largest induced events at the Poing site were
$M\sim 2.1$, with no events exceeding the German PGV $=5$~mm/s
damage threshold under their adopted monitoring framework.
\item No plant has been forced to permanently shut down on
induced-seismicity grounds; one plant (Insheim, Upper Rhine
Graben --- different geology) had operations modified after
felt events.
\end{itemize}
This empirical baseline suggests $M\sim 2$--$3$ felt events are
\emph{plausible} for an Aspern-scale Vienna~Basin doublet over a
multi-decade horizon, but $M > 4$ events are \emph{not} expected
in the hydrothermal operating regime. The expectation is heavily
conditioned on injection-pressure discipline (no high-pressure
stimulation, which is what made Basel and Pohang dangerous).

\paragraph{Proposed traffic-light system for the Aspern doublet.}
Combining (i) the Levi \emph{et al.}\ stress regime, (ii) the AFS
proximity, (iii) the Bavarian Molasse Basin operational analogues,
and (iv) the Hintersberger \& Decker (2018) [6]
palaeoseismological case that the surrounding Vienna~Basin fault
system can host $M \geq 7$ tectonic events, the defensible
operational traffic-light proposal for a Vienna Basin doublet is:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\hline
\textbf{Level} & \textbf{Trigger (within 10~km of doublet)} & \textbf{Action} \\
\hline
Green   & no event, or $M < 1.5$                  & routine monitoring \\
Amber   & $1.5 \leq M \leq 2.5$, or felt event of any size & real-time review; consider rate reduction \\
Red     & $M > 2.5$ or PGV $> 1$~mm/s at populated site & temporary shutdown, geomechanical review \\
Black   & $M > 4$ or repeat amber within 30~days  & full operational halt pending regulator review \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

These thresholds are tighter than typical EGS thresholds (which
allow up to $M \approx 3$ before red) because (i) the urban
context of Aspern raises the consequence of a felt event, and
(ii) the Hintersberger \& Decker [6] argument that
``no historical events'' does not mean the splay faults are
inactive: the tectonic-loading-rate is real even if the
recurrence time is millennia. Public-acceptance considerations
support tightening rather than relaxing.

the $M\sim 5$ events that have occurred during the instrumental

\textbf{Spatial clustering refinement (Phase~3e).} Running DBSCAN
($\varepsilon=8$~km, min\_samples$=5$) on the 160-event catalog
(notebook \texttt{scripts/seismicity\_clusters.py}) separates the
Vienna~Basin background seismicity into three clusters
(Fig.~\ref{fig:seismicity}). Critically:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{The 10 events closest to Aspern are all in cluster~C1
(Fischamend area) or are isolated/noise; \emph{none} are in C0
(Ebreichsdorf).}
\item \emph{The 10 events closest to Ebreichsdorf are all in C0.}
\item No DBSCAN cluster contains both Aspern-area and
Ebreichsdorf-area events.
\item Within the 15~km Aspern traffic-light zone there are $n=3$
events (all $M < 2.8$); within the 15~km Ebreichsdorf zone there are
$n = 50$ events (including $M\,5.3$ Moosbrunn). \emph{The two zones
do not overlap.}
\end{itemize}

This refines the T2 traffic-light proposal: the 10~km amber radius
\emph{cannot} pick up the Ebreichsdorf cluster as background, so the
amber-trigger rate would actually be lower than the $\sim 1$--$3$~/yr
basin-wide estimate. Using the Aspern-local cluster (C1) annual rate
($\sim 13$~events over 36 years, all within $\sim 30$~km of Aspern) and
narrowing to the 10~km radius, the \emph{local} background is
$\lesssim 0.2$~$M\geq 1.5$ events/yr. \emph{Any felt event within
10~km of the doublet would therefore be exceptional against the
local background, supporting the practical attribution of induced
seismicity post-startup.}

\textbf{k-means cross-check.} k-means at $k=3$ (silhouette $0.45$)
identifies one ``Aspern-area'' centroid (24 events, ~15~km from
Aspern), separate from the larger Ebreichsdorf-area centroid (76
events). The conclusion is robust to the clustering method.

\textbf{Implications for the traffic-light proposal.} At the
observed baseline of $\sim 1.35$~$M\geq 3$ events/yr in the
bbox, the proposed Amber threshold ($1.5 \leq M \leq 2.5$ within
10~km) would trigger on the order of \emph{$\sim 1$--$3$ Amber
event detections per year} just from background tectonics; this
is the appropriate operational tempo and is not over-tight.
The Red threshold ($M > 2.5$ within 10~km) would be a
\emph{rare} background trigger ($\sim 0.1$/yr at this rate),
making post-Red induced-seismicity attribution practical.

\paragraph{Falsification (what would change our conclusion).}
\begin{itemize}
\item The Levi \emph{et al.}\ [44] stress regime turns out to be
\emph{strike-slip} or \emph{reverse} rather than normal-faulting
at the Aspern target depth; the Coulomb-stress arithmetic above
would change sign and the AFS faults would be less easily
triggered.
\item Aspern injection pressures stay within $\sim 1$~MPa above
hydrostatic (much lower than typical doublet practice), in which
case the pore-pressure perturbation at km-scale collapses to
$\sim 0.01$~MPa and the triggering question becomes academic.
\item GeoSphere catalog analysis (deferred) shows that the AFS
splays have produced no $M > 1$ activity in 50+~yr of monitoring,
suggesting low criticality. This would soften the case for tight
amber/red thresholds.
\item A felt event ($M \geq 2.5$) occurs at any Bavarian Molasse
Basin plant in the next 12~months, in which case our analogue
calibration tightens and the traffic-light proposal should be
revised in real time.
\end{itemize}

\begin{itemize}
\item Build a simple Coulomb-stress / pore-pressure-diffusion model

the resulting failure-pressure perturbation against the static stress

\item Quantitative comparison Basel/Pohang vs.\ Molasse vs.\ projected Vienna
operating envelope --- a clean physics figure (injection pressure,

\item Did the decades of OMV oil and gas injection in the Vienna Basin

\item Design proposal for a microseismic traffic-light system specific
to the Vienna Basin geology, with publicly justifiable thresholds.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Thread 3 --- Lithium (and trace element) co-production}

\paragraph{State of the art.}
Co-production of critical raw materials from geothermal brine is a fast
moving field driven by the EU Critical Raw Materials Act (2024) and
recovering battery demand. The European benchmark is Vulcan Energy
Resources in the Upper Rhine Graben (URG): brines at $\sim 150$--$200$~mg/L
Li, an aluminium-sorbent direct-lithium-extraction (DLE) process, and
a EUR~2.2~billion financing package closed in December 2025.
Commercial production of $\sim 24{,}000$~t/yr lithium-hydroxide-monohydrate
(LHM) is targeted for 2028 [14, 15].

The DLE technology landscape is consolidating around three families:
aluminium-based sorbents (Eramet, Vulcan; $\sim 90\%$ recovery, operates
at native brine temperature), manganese-oxide spinel sorbents (LMO),
and lithium-ion sieve composites [16, 17]. Sorbent
lifetime remains the dominant operating-cost driver. Lithium carbonate
prices recovered sharply in early 2026 (from $\sim\$13$k/t in Dec 2025
to $\sim\$26$k/t in late January, with Chinese prices around
CNY~175{,}000/t in May 2026), and forecasts shift the market from
surplus to potential supply deficit through 2026
[18].

\paragraph{Vienna Basin specifics --- the genuine research gap.}
This thread contains the most candid research opening of the four:
\emph{no published lithium concentration data for Vienna Basin formation
waters was found in the searches conducted for this draft}. Brine
chemistry studies for the basin exist in the petroleum-geology
literature (TDS, salinity, gas composition) and the general theory of
lithium enrichment in sedimentary formation waters is well developed
[19], but Li-specific assays for OMV produced
water from the Matzen / Sch\"onkirchen / Aderklaa fields appear to
be either unpublished or buried in operator archives. The URG Li
concentrations are the product of a very specific
felsic-basement / Permo-Triassic-aquifer setting; the Pannonian-edge
Vienna Basin has a different sediment provenance and may well have
much lower Li. But until someone actually \emph{measures} this, the
question is open.

\paragraph{Open questions for the retreat.}
\begin{itemize}
\item Literature triage: produce a systematic review of all Vienna Basin
formation-water chemistry studies (GBA / GeoSphere Austria reports,
OMV production geology theses from TU Wien and Montanuniversit\"at
Leoben) and tabulate any Li, B, Cs, Rb measurements that exist.
\item Order-of-magnitude estimate: even at URG-like Li concentrations
($\sim 175$~mg/L) and Aderklaa-96-like flow rates ($\sim 40$~m$^3$/h),
what would the annual LCE output be? Is it economic at 2026 prices?
\item Geochemical-equilibrium reasoning: based on Vienna Basin host
lithologies (carbonates, Miocene clastics) and reservoir temperatures,
what Li concentration range is \emph{plausible} a priori?
\item Helium and noble gases: any indication of He enrichment in the
deep Vienna Basin that would change the co-product economics?
\end{itemize}

The retreat could plausibly produce an actual unpublished result here
(an order-of-magnitude bound on Vienna Basin Li potential), rather than
just a literature review.

\subsection*{Thread 4 --- Seasonal storage coupling (HT-ATES)}

\paragraph{State of the art.}
The seasonality of Vienna's heat demand (peak $\sim 3$~GW winter, $\sim 0.3$~GW
summer) means that constant-output geothermal is over-built for winter or
under-utilised in summer unless coupled to seasonal storage.
High-Temperature Aquifer Thermal Energy Storage (HT-ATES), at
$\geq 60\,^\circ$C and up to $\sim 90\,^\circ$C, is the technology most
likely to integrate cleanly with deep geothermal because both rely on
the same hydrogeological substrate [20]. The EU
\emph{HEATSTORE} project has supported pilot installations in
Switzerland (Bern Forsthaus, Geneva), the Netherlands and Iceland;
TU Delft operates a campus-scale HT-ATES research demonstrator;
the Burgwedel (Germany) techno-economic case study from 2024 is a
useful benchmark for HT-ATES economics in conditions resembling
Vienna Basin sediments [21].

The dominant physics issues are: (i) round-trip efficiency, typically
60--80\% and declining with storage temperature; (ii) buoyancy-driven
tilting of the thermal plume in HT regime; (iii) thermo-hydro-mechanical
coupling and small ground-surface uplift [22];
(iv) hydrochemical scaling/clogging at higher temperatures.

\paragraph{Vienna Basin specifics.}
Wien Energie has an ongoing \emph{ATES Vienna} investigation under the
Green Energy Lab umbrella, examining thermal-water reservoirs at
600--3000~m depth for seasonal district-heat storage --- explicitly
framed as a prerequisite for full DH decarbonisation [23].
The project is in an early/exploratory phase; quantitative results
on aquifer suitability, achievable storage temperatures, and
round-trip efficiency for the specific Vienna Basin aquifers are not
yet in the literature. There is therefore both clear utility interest
in the question and room for an independent academic contribution.

\paragraph{Energy-balance optimisation (Phase~2 analysis).} We
parameterise the doublet-count $N$ vs.\ storage-volume $V$
tradeoff using a seasonal block model for Vienna's demand,
calibrated to the F2 totals. We split the year into two blocks
(winter cold half $Q_1+Q_4$, $\sim 4380$~h; warm half
$Q_2+Q_3$, $\sim 4380$~h), constrained by the F2 frozen totals
(6~TWh/yr, $\sim 2.3$~GW historical winter peak [43], 0.3~GW summer
trough). A two-block
fit consistent with the published seasonality gives average
loads $D_{\mathrm{w}}\approx 1.0$~GW (winter) and
$D_{\mathrm{s}}\approx 0.37$~GW (summer), summing to 6~TWh.

Each doublet supplies constant power $P_{\mathrm{d}} = 18.8$~MW at
CF $= 0.85$ (frozen), so effective continuous output per doublet is
$P_{\mathrm{eff}} = 15.98$~MW. Geothermal alone covering year-round
demand needs

\begin{itemize}
\item to meet \emph{winter average alone}: $N \geq D_{\mathrm{w}}
/ P_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx 62.6$~doublets (still leaves the
$\sim 3$~GW winter peak uncovered);
\item to meet the entire annual energy demand: $N \geq 6000\,\mathrm{GWh}
/ (140\,\mathrm{GWh/doublet}) \approx 42.9$~doublets, but this is
\emph{only feasible with storage}, since constant output
($\sim 0.7$~GW from 43 doublets) under-covers winter and
over-covers summer.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Storage closes the seasonal gap.} Let $\eta_{\mathrm{RTE}}
= 0.7$ be the HT-ATES round-trip efficiency. Define the seasonal
energy balance:
\[
\eta_{\mathrm{RTE}} \cdot (P_{\mathrm{eff}} \cdot N - D_{\mathrm{s}}) \cdot
4380\,\mathrm{h}
\;\geq\;
(D_{\mathrm{w}} - P_{\mathrm{eff}} \cdot N) \cdot 4380\,\mathrm{h}
\]
which solves to
\[
N \geq \frac{D_{\mathrm{w}} + \eta_{\mathrm{RTE}}\, D_{\mathrm{s}}}
              {P_{\mathrm{eff}}(1 + \eta_{\mathrm{RTE}})}
       = \frac{1000 + 0.7 \cdot 370}{15.98 \cdot 1.7}
       \approx 46.0\;\mathrm{doublets.}
\]
\emph{So roughly $N \approx 46$ doublets with seasonal storage
satisfy the energy-balance constraint, vs.\ $\sim 100$+
doublets if peak-sized without storage.} Storage saves on the
order of 50~doublets, i.e.\ saves $\sim 50 \times 26$~M $\approx$
EUR~1.3~B in subsurface capital, against the cost of storage
($V$ to be computed).

\textbf{Storage volume requirement (the reality check).} The
peak stored energy occurs at the summer/winter inflection. For
$N=46$ doublets, summer surplus is
$(15.98 \cdot 46 - 370) \cdot 4380 = 363 \cdot 4380 \approx
1{,}590{,}000$~MWh of heat stored. At $\Delta T = 60$~K and
water-equivalent volumetric heat capacity $c_p \rho = 4.18 \cdot
10^6$~J/(m$^3$\,K):
\[
V_{\mathrm{store}} = \frac{E}{c_p\rho \Delta T}
                   = \frac{1.59 \cdot 10^{12}\,\mathrm{Wh} \cdot 3600}
                          {4.18 \cdot 10^6 \cdot 60}
                   \approx 2.3 \cdot 10^7\;\mathrm{m^3}.
\]
\emph{This is the headline reality check}: a 23~million~m$^3$
storage is about $115\times$ the largest existing PTES (Vojens
Denmark, $\sim 200{,}000$~m$^3$) and is well outside the range of
any operational HT-ATES today. Even an aquifer at industrial
scale rarely operates beyond $\sim 10^6$~m$^3$; we are
two orders of magnitude beyond that.

\textbf{What this means.} Full seasonal closure by storage alone
is not realistic for Vienna at near-term horizons. The defensible
mode is \emph{partial} storage coupling: storage to smooth
shoulder-season variability and reduce the marginal winter
peakers, not to replace them.

\paragraph{LDC-based refinement (Phase~3c).} The two-block model
above is honest about being a rough approximation. With access to
the actual Wien Energie daily load-duration curve from
Schubert (2022) [48] (Compass Lexecon 2021 underlying study), we
re-ran the same optimisation using a 365-day digitised LDC. The
notebook \texttt{notebooks/t4\_storage\_ldc.py} contains the
30-sample digitisation and the analysis. Headline differences:

\begin{center}

\hline
\textbf{Quantity} & \textbf{Two-block} & \textbf{LDC-based} \\
\hline

\emph{The two-block model dramatically over-estimated storage
volume} (by a factor of $\sim 100$) because half-year-block
averaging missed the fact that the LDC is \emph{narrow at the
peak} (only $\sim 30$ days exceed 30~GWh/d) and \emph{long-tailed
at the bottom} (most days are at modest demand). A modest
geothermal baseload ($\sim 39$ doublets) covers most of the LDC
area, with only a few weeks of summer surplus to be stored.
\textbf{This is the most important quantitative correction the
reproducibility loop produced for this case study.}

\textbf{Caveat: this assumes ideal hourly storage charge/discharge
within the day.} Real HT-ATES has inter-day inertia (hours to charge
or discharge), so a fully dispatchable model would need finer-than-
daily resolution. The 0.2 million~m$^3$ headline is therefore a
\emph{lower bound}; a more realistic operational target (covering
intra-week variability) is probably in the $1$--$5$ million~m$^3$
range, still 5--20$\times$ smaller than the two-block estimate.

\textbf{Note on the demand basis.} The Schubert (2022) LDC is a
\emph{2040 projection} that incorporates Vienna's building-efficiency
roadmap; the annual total of 4.9~TWh is below the current 6~TWh
because off-peak demand falls with insulation upgrades, while the
peak hour ($\sim 2308$~MW current vs.\ $\sim 2375$~MW projected)
is roughly maintained because peak demand is set by cold-snap
building heat loss. For a geothermal plant commissioning in 2028
and operating to $\sim 2058$, the 2040-projection basis is the
more relevant planning anchor than the current-demand basis.

\paragraph{Partial-coverage scenario (the realistic operating point).}
A more realistic target is to dimension geothermal+storage so it
covers $X\%$ of annual demand, with biomass/HP/gas peakers
covering the rest. For $X=60\%$ (in line with Wien Energie's
implicit 2040 plan of $\sim 4$~TWh/yr from HP + geothermal):
$N\approx 60\% \cdot 42.9 = 26$~doublets and a storage of
$\sim 4$--$6$~million~m$^3$ (still ambitious but within
near-term plausibility for aquifer storage at multi-site scale).
This converges on the same order of magnitude as
Wien Energie's stated $\sim 120$~MW (= $\sim 7$~doublets) by
2030 followed by deeper deployment toward 2040 [1]. The
quantitative analysis above provides a check on this trajectory.

Winter avg $D_{\mathrm{w}}=1.3$~GW (colder)     & 57  & 34 \\
60\% coverage target                            & 26  & $\sim 2.6$ \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The dominant lever is $\Delta T$: doubling the storage operating
temperature lift halves the volume. This is why HT-ATES (60--90
$^\circ$C delivered) is genuinely transformative compared with
low-temperature ATES (15--30 $^\circ$C delivered, $\Delta T \sim
15$~K, requiring $\sim 100$~million~m$^3$ for the same energy).

\paragraph{Falsification (what would change our conclusion).}
\begin{itemize}
\item Wien Energie's actual hourly demand curve has a much
narrower winter-summer envelope than the 2.7$\times$ assumed
here (e.g.\ if DHW load dominates over space heating year-round,
the storage requirement could fall substantially). Resolving this
requires data we do not yet have.
\item HT-ATES round-trip efficiency in real Vienna Basin aquifers
turns out to be $\ll 50\%$ owing to scaling, thermal plume
tilting, or fault-bounded flow paths. This would push the
optimal mix to lower-storage, more-peaker configurations.
\item Drilling-cost inflation pushes per-doublet CapEx above
$\sim 40$~M EUR, in which case storage-vs-extra-doublet trades
in favour of storage even at the volumes above.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Open questions for the retreat.}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Headline optimisation problem.} Given Vienna's annual
heat-demand curve (well known) and a parameterised HT-ATES (storage
volume $V$, storage temperature $T_{\mathrm{st}}$, round-trip
efficiency $\eta(T_{\mathrm{st}})$), what combination of geothermal
doublet count $N$ and storage volume $V$ minimises the levelised
cost of heat (LCOH) for a given decarbonisation target? This is a
clean, retreat-sized optimisation problem with publishable potential.
\item Heat-loss scaling: confirm that the surface-to-volume scaling
for an HT-ATES at Vienna-relevant scales gives the round-trip
efficiencies cited in HEATSTORE, or identify the regime where it
does not.
\item Thermal-plume migration and buoyancy: under what aquifer
parameters does buoyancy-driven tilting become a first-order loss
mechanism? A 2-D simulation with realistic Vienna Basin permeability
contrasts would be illuminating.
\item Alternative storage technologies for comparison: pit thermal
energy storage (PTES, cf.\ Danish Vojens at $\sim 200{,}000$~m$^3$)
and large-scale heat pumps with thermochemical or hydrogen storage
--- is HT-ATES actually the right answer for Vienna?
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Thread 5 --- Geothermal heat vs.\ large heat pumps + green electricity}

\paragraph{Why this is the question Wien Energie can't answer for itself.}
Wien Energie is already operating Europe's most powerful large-scale heat
pump --- a 55~MW$_{\mathrm{th}}$ wastewater-source plant at the Ebswien
treatment site near Simmering, commissioned 2023, which lifts wastewater
from $\sim 11$--$12\,^\circ$C in winter (24--$25\,^\circ$C summer) to
$\sim 90\,^\circ$C feed temperature. The plant currently supplies
$\sim 56{,}000$ households; a second stage doubling capacity to
$110$~MW$_{\mathrm{th}}$ (target $\sim 112{,}000$ households) is scheduled
for completion in 2027 [24, 25]. Capital cost of stage 1 was
$\sim$EUR~70~million, i.e.\ $\sim$EUR~1.3~million per MW$_{\mathrm{th}}$
installed. \emph{The same utility is therefore simultaneously building
deep geothermal and large heat pumps to decarbonise the same district-heat
network.} The implicit question --- in what mix --- is not addressed in any
public-facing document we have located, and the academic literature on
direct LCOH/LCOA comparisons for large-DH applications is surprisingly
thin [26].

\paragraph{The physics-level comparison.}
The two technologies sit at very different points of the
exergy/capex/opex Pareto front:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Deep geothermal doublet:} CapEx $\sim$EUR~20--35~M per
$\sim 18$~MW$_{\mathrm{th}}$ doublet (i.e.\ $\sim$EUR~1.4~M/MW), capacity
factor $\sim 85$--$90\%$, almost zero operating energy input, $\sim 30$~yr
nominal lifetime with depletion physics (see thread 7), small marginal
cost per MWh.
\item \textbf{Large heat pump:} CapEx $\sim$EUR~1.3~M/MW based on Vienna's
own numbers [24], COP $\sim 3$--$3.5$ for wastewater-source $\to 90\,^\circ$C
delivery, so electricity input $\sim 0.3$~MWh per MWh of heat delivered.
At an Austrian grid carbon intensity of $\sim 100$~g~CO$_2$/kWh
(hydropower-dominated) this is essentially low-carbon today; whether it
stays low-carbon depends entirely on the marginal-electricity assumption
during winter peak.
\end{itemize}
The crossover is dominated not by capex (similar per-MW) but by
\emph{electricity-cost expectations} and \emph{carbon-intensity of
marginal electricity}. Both are policy- and weather-dependent in ways
that make sensitivity analysis essential.

\paragraph{LCOH calculation (Phase~2 analysis).} We now compute the
levelised cost of heat (LCOH, in EUR/MWh$_{\mathrm{th}}$) for both
technologies under shared, transparent assumptions. The formula is
the standard annuity model:
\[
\mathrm{LCOH} =
\frac{\mathrm{CapEx}\cdot\mathrm{CRF} + \mathrm{OpEx_{fix}} + \mathrm{OpEx_{elec}}}
     {P\cdot\mathrm{CF}\cdot 8760\,\mathrm{h}}
\]
with capital recovery factor
$\mathrm{CRF} = r(1+r)^n / ((1+r)^n - 1)$ for discount rate $r$ and
plant lifetime $n$. Inputs (assumptions table):

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lllll}
\hline
\textbf{Parameter} & \textbf{Geothermal} & \textbf{Heat pump} & \textbf{Source} & \textbf{Conf.} \\
\hline
CapEx (per MW$_{\mathrm{th}}$) & 1.4~M EUR & 1.3~M EUR & F2; [24] & A,A \\
Lifetime $n$                  & 30~yr     & 20~yr     & lit.\ norm & B,B \\
Discount rate $r$             & 5\%       & 5\%       & EU norm    & A,A \\
CRF                           & 0.0651    & 0.0802    & derived    & C \\
Fixed OpEx (\% CapEx/yr)      & 2.5\%     & 5.0\%     & lit.\ norm & B,B \\
COP                           & ---       & 3.2       & [24]       & A \\
Capacity factor (CF)          & 0.85      & 0.50      & frozen / est.& A,D \\
Reference unit thermal power  & 18.8~MW   & 55~MW     & F2 frozen  & A \\
Electricity price (base case) & ---       & 90~EUR/MWh & F3 mid     & A \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\textbf{Geothermal doublet (Aspern-type, base case).} CapEx
$= 1.4 \times 18.8 = 26.3$~M EUR; annualised
$= 26.3 \times 0.0651 = 1.71$~M EUR/yr; fixed OpEx
$= 0.025 \times 26.3 = 0.66$~M EUR/yr; total
$2.37$~M EUR/yr. Annual heat output
$= 18.8 \times 8760 \times 0.85 = 140{,}010$~MWh.
\[
\mathrm{LCOH_{geo}} \approx \frac{2.37 \times 10^6}{140{,}010}
       \approx 16.9 \mathrm{~EUR/MWh_{th}}\quad\textbf{(headline)}
\]

\textbf{Large heat pump (Ebswien-type, base case).} CapEx
$= 1.3 \times 55 = 71.5$~M EUR; annualised
$= 71.5 \times 0.0802 = 5.74$~M EUR/yr; fixed OpEx
$= 0.05 \times 71.5 = 3.58$~M EUR/yr. Annual heat
$= 55 \times 8760 \times 0.50 = 240{,}900$~MWh; electricity input
$= 240{,}900 / 3.2 = 75{,}281$~MWh; cost
$= 75{,}281 \times 90 / 10^6 = 6.78$~M EUR/yr. Total
$5.74 + 3.58 + 6.78 = 16.10$~M EUR/yr.
\[
\mathrm{LCOH_{HP}} \approx \frac{16.10 \times 10^6}{240{,}900}
       \approx 66.8 \mathrm{~EUR/MWh_{th}}\quad\textbf{(headline)}
\]

\textbf{Base-case headline:} geothermal LCOH $\sim 17$~EUR/MWh$_{\mathrm{th}}$
\emph{vs.}\ heat pump $\sim 67$~EUR/MWh$_{\mathrm{th}}$ at the
\emph{midpoint} inputs --- a factor $\sim 4$ ratio. However, taking the
F2-frozen geothermal CapEx range (1.06--1.86~M~EUR/MW, i.e.\ 20--35~M EUR
per doublet) rather than just the midpoint, the geothermal LCOH ranges
$\sim 13$--$22$~EUR/MWh; combined with sensible CF and OpEx adjustments,
the realistic geothermal LCOH range is $\sim 13$--$28$~EUR/MWh and the
geo:HP ratio is $\sim 2.4$--$5\times$. Reporting only the
midpoint as the headline is misleading; the \textbf{honest headline is
``geothermal is roughly $2$--$5\times$ cheaper than the
Ebswien-class heat pump under defensible input ranges''} [conf.\ C].

\paragraph{Curtailment-aware CF (Phase~3c).} The base-case
$\mathrm{CF}_{\mathrm{geo}}=0.85$ implicitly assumes that all
geothermal output is utilised when produced --- i.e.\ that summer
surplus is absorbed by storage. The LDC analysis in thread~4
shows that \emph{without} storage, geothermal output above demand
must be curtailed during the LDC's low-demand tail. Effective CF
under curtailment, from the LDC notebook
\texttt{t4\_storage\_ldc.py}, is:

\textbf{N (doublets)} & \textbf{CF if all output utilised} & \textbf{CF curtailed (no storage)} \\
\hline
10  & 0.85 & 0.84 \\
20  & 0.85 & 0.74 \\

For a 46-doublet fleet \emph{without} seasonal storage, $\mathrm{CF}

\paragraph{HP-weighted electricity price refinement (Phase~3g addition).}
The T5 base case uses a flat $90$~EUR/MWh wholesale electricity input.
The Austrian hourly Ember dataset [42] (\texttt{scripts/austria\_price\_stats.py},
99{,}788 hourly points 2015--2026) reveals a systematic
\emph{HP-weighted} premium because heat pumps consume electricity in
winter evenings, when prices are highest:

(midpoint 17~EUR/MWh) is unaffected since deep geothermal doesn't
consume grid electricity. \emph{The geo:HP ratio therefore widens
under the realistic time-weighted price} from $\sim 4\times$ to

geothermal-CapEx range). Diurnal price pattern confirms the
mechanism: 2024--2025 hour-19 local average is 130~EUR/MWh,
hour-13 local is 56~EUR/MWh --- a $2.3\times$ peak/trough ratio that
HPs cannot avoid because their thermal output must coincide with
demand. The 2025 winter average (119~EUR/MWh) is the most aggressive
sustained price an HP would face in operation, and even there the
geothermal advantage holds.

\paragraph{Gas-CHP counterfactual and EU ETS (Phase~3f addition).}
The geo--HP comparison above omits the \emph{incumbent} technology
being displaced: gas combined heat and power, which supplies
$\sim 52\%$ of Vienna's current DH (F2). A defensible decarbonisation
case must compare both clean options against the gas baseline.
Gas-CHP delivers heat at $\sim 35$--$60$~EUR/MWh$_{\mathrm{th}}$
under TTF gas at 35--50~EUR/MWh and EU~ETS at 80--100~EUR/tCO$_2$
(2026 forwards), and emits $\sim 200$~g~CO$_2$/kWh$_{\mathrm{th}}$.
At EU~ETS = 90~EUR/tCO$_2$, the avoided-carbon credit for either
geothermal or low-carbon HP is $\approx 200 \cdot 10^{-3}
\cdot 90 = 18$~EUR/MWh$_{\mathrm{th}}$ --- comparable in magnitude
to the geothermal LCOH itself. Carbon-credited:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Geothermal:} $\sim 17 - 18 \approx -1$~EUR/MWh
(net positive economic case before ETS pass-through).
\item \textbf{Heat pump (Austrian grid):} $\sim 67 - (18 - 7) =
\sim 56$~EUR/MWh (smaller credit since HP itself emits
$\sim 33$~g~CO$_2$/kWh life-cycle).
\item \textbf{Gas-CHP at current ETS:} $\sim 35$--$60$~EUR/MWh.
\end{itemize}
Under reasonable EU~ETS price paths, geothermal is competitive
with gas-CHP \emph{before} subsidies, and heat pumps are not.
This re-frames the policy case considerably: heat pumps are
attractive on dispatch and risk grounds; geothermal is
attractive on long-run unit economics including the carbon
externality. The two complement each other rather than
compete --- which is exactly what Wien~Energie's portfolio
implies.

\paragraph{Why is Wien Energie hedging? Three plausible explanations.}
The base-case factor-4 LCOH advantage makes the
``Wien~Energie should just build geothermal'' inference seductive.
We see three reasons not to trust it as policy advice:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Portfolio and dispatch risk.} A single drilling
failure on a planned doublet leaves a $\sim$EUR~25~M hole and
$\sim 140$~GWh/yr of heat undelivered. Heat pumps are modular
($\sim 18$~MW each) and have no subsurface risk: capex per
delivered MWh is higher but the loss distribution is
narrower.
\item \textbf{Lead time.} A Vienna Basin doublet is a multi-year
project (drilling started 2024 at Aspern for 2028 commissioning
[1]). A heat pump can be ordered, installed, and commissioned in
$\sim$~18 months [24]. The optionality value of heat pumps
during the 2025--2030 ramp is real even if their long-run LCOH
is higher.
\item \textbf{Heat-source thermal headroom.} Heat-pump LCOH is
strongly sensitive to source temperature: wastewater is good,
ambient air is much worse. Vienna's wastewater stream is
finite (Ebswien capacity hard-cap), so heat pump scaling
faces a sink-side bottleneck the geothermal case does not.
\end{itemize}
A defensible policy position --- one we believe the retreat
should articulate --- is therefore: \emph{long-run, geothermal is
the cheaper bulk heat source; near-term, heat pumps cover ramp
risk; the optimal mix is decided by drilling-success rate and
heat-pump-source availability, not by carbon}.

\paragraph{Falsification (what would change our conclusion).}
The base-case ordering breaks if any of the following turn out to
be true:
\begin{itemize}
\item Geothermal doublet success rate is materially below
$\sim 80\%$ at Aspern-type conditions (a 30\% dry-well rate alone
adds $\sim$EUR~7--10/MWh to the geothermal LCOH; combined with
other adverse parameters this can begin to close the gap).
\item Vienna Basin geothermal capacity factors are systematically
overestimated (e.g.\ depletion is faster than thread~7 estimates
predict, dropping average CF to $\sim 0.60$ within 15~yr).
\item Heat pumps achieve sustained electricity sourcing at
nearly-zero or negative wholesale prices (extreme renewables
overbuild) for $> 50\%$ of operating hours.
\item Drilling-cost inflation (which has been substantial
post-2022) pushes CapEx beyond 50~M~EUR per doublet, while heat
pump capex stays flat.
\end{itemize}
None of these is implausible in isolation. The honest position is
that geothermal has a strong central-case advantage but a wider
distribution than a single number suggests; reporting the LCOH
as ``17~EUR/MWh'' without the sensitivity table would be
misleading.

\paragraph{Open questions for the retreat.}
\begin{itemize}
\item Least-cost optimisation over Vienna's annual DH load curve, with
both technologies plus biomass + existing waste-incineration (Spittelau,
already in the network) + HT-ATES (thread 4) competing for share.
What does the optimum mix actually look like?
\item Sensitivity to electricity price (EUR~50--200/MWh) and marginal
carbon intensity (50--500~g~CO$_2$/kWh) --- which of these flips the
ordering?
\item Why is Wien Energie hedging? Is it a portfolio-risk argument
(no single technology covers all of Vienna's $\sim 3$~GW winter peak),
or a thermal-temperature argument (heat pumps marginal for 90~$^\circ$C
output, geothermal at native temperature)?
\item Land use, water use, public acceptance --- secondary axes that
the retreat could quantify rather than just qualitatively note.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Thread 6 --- CO$_2$ (and supercritical) working fluids in closed-loop geothermal}

\paragraph{Why this just became urgent.}
On 4 December 2025, Eavor Technologies delivered its first grid
electricity from the Geretsried (Bavaria) closed-loop multilateral
system --- the first commercial demonstration of an Eavor-Loop at scale
[27, 28]. The Geretsried plant comprises two vertical wells with six
horizontal laterals sidetracked from each, the laterals connected
toe-to-toe underground using active magnetic ranging, forming a
$\sim 12$-lateral subsurface radiator designed for $64$~MW$_{\mathrm{th}}$
and $8.2$~MW$_{\mathrm{e}}$. Crucially, the thermosiphon effect was
established within $\sim 30$ minutes of start-up and the system now
runs pump-free [28]. This is the first time the closed-loop thermosiphon
geometry --- proposed for working fluids including supercritical
CO$_2$ since Brown (2000) --- has been operationally demonstrated as
a power source.

\paragraph{The CO$_2$-versus-water question.}
Eavor uses water; the deeper question is whether supercritical CO$_2$
($T_c = 31.1\,^\circ$C, $p_c = 7.4$~MPa) is a better working fluid at
Vienna-Basin-relevant temperatures. The physics arguments for sCO$_2$:
\begin{itemize}
\item Steeper density gradient with temperature than water in the
relevant regime, hence stronger thermosiphon driving force and lower
parasitic pumping load;
\item Lower viscosity, higher mass flow per unit pressure drop;
\item Potential for higher conversion efficiency in direct-cycle
turbomachinery (Brayton cycle on sCO$_2$);
\item In the open-loop variant (CO$_2$ Plume Geothermal, CPG), permanent
sequestration of CO$_2$ in the host sedimentary basin as a co-benefit [29].
\end{itemize}
The physics arguments against: corrosivity (limits material choices),
high operating pressure ($> 10$~MPa typical), and --- in CPG mode ---
displacement of native brine into the wellbore and potential reactivity
with carbonate host rocks (e.g.\ the Aderklaa Hauptdolomit). For closed
loops, leakage of CO$_2$ to atmosphere defeats the climate purpose.

\paragraph{Vienna Basin specifics.}
No published Vienna Basin sCO$_2$ work has been located in the searches
conducted here. The most concrete physics-flavoured connection is the
overlap with thread 1: the Aderklaa-96 retrofit is, in principle, a
candidate for either a coaxial-water BHE or a single-well sCO$_2$
thermosiphon trial. The Hauptdolomit carbonate host raises the CPG
reactivity question concretely. Multi-lateral Eavor-style geometries are
probably not retrofittable into legacy vertical OMV wells without major
sidetracking work, but the toe-to-toe lateral connection technology is
now demonstrated at Geretsried and could in principle be applied
greenfield in the Vienna Basin. \emph{Note}: Eavor's Geretsried system
itself uses a water-based working fluid [27], not sCO$_2$; the sCO$_2$
variant remains experimental.

\paragraph{sCO$_2$ vs.\ water at Aderklaa-96 conditions ---
Phase~2 thermodynamic calculation.} CO$_2$ becomes supercritical above
$T_c = 30.98\,^\circ$C and $P_c = 7.39$~MPa [45]. At the Aderklaa-96
bottomhole conditions ($T\approx 102\,^\circ$C, $P\approx 28$~MPa
assuming a hydrostatic column to $2900$~m), CO$_2$ sits well into the
supercritical region; the surface return ($T\approx 30\,^\circ$C,
$P\approx 7$--$10$~MPa) is just at the critical envelope, which is
where the strongest density gradient with temperature occurs ---
exactly the regime where the thermosiphon advantage shows up. Approximate
fluid properties at Aderklaa-96 operating conditions:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lllll}
\hline
\textbf{Property} & \multicolumn{2}{c}{\textbf{sCO$_2$}} & \multicolumn{2}{c}{\textbf{H$_2$O}} \\
                  & cold (30~$^\circ$C)& hot (102~$^\circ$C)& cold & hot \\
\hline
$\rho$ [kg/m$^3$]              & $\sim 880$ & $\sim 700$ & $\sim 996$ & $\sim 957$ \\
$\Delta\rho$ (hot$\to$cold)    & \multicolumn{2}{c}{$\sim 180$ kg/m$^3$} & \multicolumn{2}{c}{$\sim 39$ kg/m$^3$} \\
$c_p$ [kJ/(kg$\cdot$K)]        & $\sim 2.2$ & $\sim 1.3$ & $4.18$     & $4.21$ \\
$\mu$ [$\mu$Pa$\cdot$s]        & $\sim 80$  & $\sim 50$  & $800$      & $280$ \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

(All values are illustrative engineering estimates from
published equation-of-state data [45]; precision is sufficient for
order-of-magnitude comparison only.)

\paragraph{Thermosiphon driving pressure.} The hydrostatic
pressure differential between the cold injection column and the
warm production column drives natural circulation. For a closed
loop with depth $H = 2900$~m and column-averaged density difference
$\overline{\Delta\rho}$:
\[
\Delta P_{\mathrm{th}} \approx \overline{\Delta\rho}\,g\,H
\]
\begin{itemize}
\item sCO$_2$: $\Delta P_{\mathrm{th}} \approx 180 \cdot 9.81
\cdot 2900 \approx 5.1$~MPa
\item H$_2$O: $\Delta P_{\mathrm{th}} \approx 39 \cdot 9.81
\cdot 2900 \approx 1.1$~MPa
\end{itemize}
The sCO$_2$ thermosiphon driving pressure is therefore
\emph{$\sim 4.6\times$ that of water} at Aderklaa-96 conditions,
which is the headline physics advantage and the reason this
working-fluid choice has been explored since Brown (2000).

\paragraph{Heat extraction per cycle.} For thermal output we
need $\dot m\,c_p\,\Delta T$. At equal mass flow:
\begin{itemize}
\item sCO$_2$: $\dot m \cdot 1.75 \cdot 72 \approx 126\,\dot m$
W (with $c_p$ averaged over the column, $\Delta T = 72$~K)
\item H$_2$O: $\dot m \cdot 4.18 \cdot 72 \approx 301\,\dot m$ W
\end{itemize}
Per unit mass flow, water extracts $\sim 2.4\times$ more heat
than sCO$_2$ over the same temperature drop --- because water has
the higher specific heat capacity. \emph{But} sCO$_2$ achieves
higher volumetric flow at any given pressure drop (lower viscosity
and higher allowable mass flux), and avoids the need for surface
pumping. The relevant net comparison is heat-per-unit-pumping-energy,
not heat-per-unit-mass-flow.

\paragraph{Net thermal output (rough Aderklaa-96 estimate).}
Combining the thermosiphon driving pressure with the heat-extraction
per cycle, a quasi-steady-state closed-loop estimate for an
Aderklaa-96 retrofit (single coaxial well, no pump) gives, for
each working fluid:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{sCO$_2$:} self-driven thermosiphon delivers
$\dot m \sim 50$--$80$~kg/s through the well at $\sim 5$~MPa
driving pressure; net heat output $\sim 6$--$10$~MW$_{\mathrm{th}}$
in transient operation, settling to $\sim 0.5$--$2$~MW$_{\mathrm{th}}$
at long-term steady state (BHE-limited contact area).
\item \textbf{H$_2$O (closed-loop coaxial):} $\sim 0.6$--$2$~MW$_{\mathrm{th}}$
steady state (Mol/Hinton case studies [12, 13]), \emph{requires
surface pumping} ($\sim 100$--$200$~kW parasitic).
\end{itemize}
The sCO$_2$ closed-loop has a real but modest steady-state
advantage at Vienna Basin temperatures; the much larger
transient advantage matters mainly for plants that ramp
($\sim$hours timescale).

\paragraph{Power conversion: Brayton vs.\ ORC.} For
\emph{electricity} (not heat) output, the case for sCO$_2$ rests
on direct-cycle Brayton-cycle conversion at the surface. Cycle
efficiency at $T_{\mathrm{in}} = 100\,^\circ$C is:
\begin{itemize}
\item sCO$_2$ recompression Brayton cycle: $\eta \approx
8$--$12\%$ at these temperatures (the strong advantage of sCO$_2$
Brayton appears at $T \geq 250\,^\circ$C, where $\eta \to 50\%$).
\item Organic Rankine Cycle (water/working-fluid hybrid): $\eta
\approx 7$--$10\%$ at the same temperatures.
\end{itemize}
At Vienna Basin temperatures, there is \emph{no clear
electricity-cycle advantage} for sCO$_2$. The technology becomes
attractive at higher-T resources (Larderello, Kenya), not at the
$100$--$130\,^\circ$C regime that Vienna offers.

\paragraph{Material compatibility and CPG complications.} An
open-loop CO$_2$ Plume Geothermal (CPG, [29]) variant for the
Vienna Basin would inject sCO$_2$ into the Hauptdolomit reservoir
to displace native brine. The dolomite host raises a specific
chemistry concern: at reservoir T and P, sCO$_2$ + residual
brine forms carbonic acid which can rapidly dissolve carbonate
rock. Dissolution alters permeability (potentially destructive
in unpredictable ways), generates excess porosity (good for CO$_2$
storage but bad for predictable heat extraction), and corrodes
carbon-steel casing (effectively all OMV legacy tubing). A CPG
variant in the Aderklaa Conglomerate (clastic, lower carbonate
content) would be less aggressive but raises the same casing
question. For a closed-loop variant, CO$_2$ never contacts
formation rock, but contact with carbon-steel tubing under
sCO$_2$ + trace water is still a known corrosion mechanism
requiring corrosion-resistant-alloy (CRA) tubing --- $\sim 3$--$5\times$
the cost of conventional carbon steel.

\paragraph{Summary judgement for Vienna Basin.} The physics of
sCO$_2$ as a working fluid is genuinely better than water at
Aderklaa-96 conditions for the thermosiphon driving force.
However, three things weaken the case in practice:
(i) heat capacity per unit mass is lower, partially offsetting
the volumetric advantage; (ii) Brayton-cycle electricity
conversion shows no advantage at modest temperatures; (iii)
material costs (CRA tubing) and reactivity concerns
(carbonate dissolution in any open-loop variant) increase capex
significantly. The net assessment: \emph{sCO$_2$ is interesting
physics for the Vienna Basin but not an obvious technology
choice in 2026}. Eavor's choice of a water-based working fluid
at Geretsried [27] --- a higher-temperature site than Aspern ---
is consistent with this assessment.

\paragraph{Falsification (what would change our conclusion).}
\begin{itemize}
\item A working sCO$_2$ closed-loop demonstration at $\leq
130\,^\circ$C source temperature reports $\geq 5$~MW$_{\mathrm{th}}$
sustained net output. (This would substantially change the
single-well closed-loop ranking.)
\item CRA tubing prices fall by $\geq 50\%$ owing to scale
deployment or new alloys, closing the material-cost gap with
carbon steel.
\item A Brayton-cycle plant at $T \approx 130\,^\circ$C achieves
$\geq 15\%$ conversion efficiency in field operation, which would
make CHP-style sCO$_2$ economically competitive at Vienna Basin
temperatures.
\item Vienna Basin reservoir characterisation reveals a deeper,
hotter target ($T \geq 180\,^\circ$C) accessible from existing
or new wells, in which case the thread~6 thermodynamic argument
becomes much stronger.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Open questions for the retreat.}
\begin{itemize}
\item Comparative thermodynamic model: water vs.\ sCO$_2$ in a coaxial
closed-loop at Aderklaa-96 conditions ($T_{\mathrm{prod}}\sim 100$\,$^\circ$C,
$\sim 3$~km depth). Quantify the thermosiphon strength and net power
delivered for each.
\item Brayton-cycle conversion efficiency for sCO$_2$ at the modest
temperatures of the Vienna Basin --- is the efficiency advantage real
at $T_{\mathrm{prod}}<150\,^\circ$C, or does it only show up at the
$\geq 200\,^\circ$C Eavor-style EGS regime?
\item CPG variant: rock--CO$_2$--brine geochemistry in the Aderklaa
Conglomerate and Hauptdolomit --- back-of-envelope reactivity calculation
to identify show-stoppers.
\item Material compatibility of OMV legacy casings (carbon steel, various
vintages) with sCO$_2$ at $\sim 100\,^\circ$C and $\sim 10$~MPa ---
literature review with quantitative degradation rates.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Thread 7 --- Long-term reservoir depletion and multi-doublet interference}

\paragraph{Why this matters for the 40-doublet vision.}
The $\sim 40$ doublets implied by our heat-balance estimate are not
hydraulically independent. Each injection well establishes a thermal plume
in the aquifer that grows with time; eventually the cold front reaches
the production well (``thermal breakthrough''), the produced temperature
declines, and the doublet must be re-sited, re-pointed, or sidetracked.
For Vienna's $\sim 30$-year deployment horizon, the doublet count is
therefore not the only design variable --- the time-evolution of the
reservoir is.

\paragraph{The empirical baseline: Dogger aquifer, Paris Basin.}
The single best dataset for low-enthalpy doublet behaviour over a
human-relevant timescale is the Dogger aquifer in the \^Ile-de-France
($\sim 1500$~m depth, $\sim 60$--$80\,^\circ$C), where district-heating
doublets have been operating continuously since the 1970s. The Lopez
\emph{et al.}\ (2010) retrospective on 40 years of operation [30]
documents the headline result: \emph{none of the active doublets has
shown a statistically significant production-temperature decline} to
date, although modelling clearly shows that the natural heat flux at
the doublet scale is insufficient to maintain current withdrawal rates
indefinitely. A 2024 reanalysis of the Dogger production data with
modern thermo-hydraulic codes [31] confirms that the operationally
relevant decline regime begins at $\sim 50$--$70$~yr for typical
well spacings of 1--1.5~km.

\paragraph{The physics one layer down.}
At doublet scale the thermal breakthrough time scales (roughly) as
$t_{\mathrm{TB}} \propto \frac{(\rho c)_{\mathrm{rock}}\, h\, L^2}{(\rho c)_{\mathrm{water}}\, Q}$
for spacing $L$, aquifer thickness $h$, and flow rate $Q$. This is a
simple analytical model the retreat could parameterise for the
Aderklaa Conglomerate and the Hauptdolomit. The interesting complications
are: (i) facies heterogeneity --- the conglomerate is not a uniform slab;
(ii) cooling-induced poroelastic stress changes large enough to reactivate
distant faults [32], which couples this thread directly to thread 2; and
(iii) cumulative thermal interference between $\sim 40$ doublets across
the Vienna Basin once deployment is mature. In the CO$_2$ Plume Geothermal
variant (thread 6), heat depletion follows different physics again [29],
making CPG potentially more or less sustainable than water doublets
depending on parameters.

\paragraph{Analytical breakthrough estimate for Aspern (Phase~2).}
Using the Bodvarsson--Tsang (1982) closed-form estimate for thermal
breakthrough in a doublet with circulation between two parallel wells
in a confined aquifer:
\[
t_{\mathrm{TB}} \;\approx\;
\frac{V_{\mathrm{swept}}\,(\rho c)_{\mathrm{bulk}}}
     {3\,(\rho c)_{\mathrm{w}}\,Q}, \qquad
V_{\mathrm{swept}} = \pi L^{2} h,
\]
where $L$ is doublet spacing, $h$ aquifer thickness, $Q$ volumetric
flow rate, and the factor of~3 in the denominator is the streamline
geometric correction. The bulk volumetric heat capacity is
$(\rho c)_{\mathrm{bulk}} = \phi (\rho c)_{\mathrm{w}} +
(1-\phi)(\rho c)_{\mathrm{r}}$ for porosity $\phi$.

Plugging in F1 parameters for an Aspern-type doublet in the
Aderklaa Conglomerate ($\phi = 0.20$, $h = 100$~m, $L = 1500$~m,
$(\rho c)_{\mathrm{r}} = 2.2\cdot 10^{6}$~J/(m$^3$\,K), $(\rho c)_{\mathrm{w}}
= 4.18\cdot 10^{6}$~J/(m$^3$\,K), $Q = 0.075$~m$^3$/s from the
F2/frozen value of $75$~kg/s):

\[
(\rho c)_{\mathrm{bulk}} = 0.20 \cdot 4.18 + 0.80 \cdot 2.20
= 2.60 \cdot 10^{6}\;\mathrm{J/(m^3\,K)},
\]
\[
V_{\mathrm{swept}} = \pi \cdot (1500)^{2} \cdot 100
= 7.07 \cdot 10^{8}\;\mathrm{m^3},
\]
\[
t_{\mathrm{TB}} = \frac{7.07\cdot 10^{8} \cdot 2.60\cdot 10^{6}}
                       {3\cdot 4.18\cdot 10^{6}\cdot 0.075}
\approx 1.95\cdot 10^{9}\;\mathrm{s}
\approx 62\;\mathrm{yr}\quad\textbf{(headline)}.
\]

\textbf{Cross-check against Dogger.} The Lopez \emph{et al.}\ (2010) [30]
result is that no Dogger doublet (operating since the 1970s with typical
$L\sim 1$~km, $h\sim 20$~m, $Q\sim 0.05$--$0.07$~m$^3$/s in a fractured
carbonate) has shown a statistically significant production-temperature
decline in 40+ years. Applying our same formula to typical Dogger
parameters gives $t_{\mathrm{TB}} \sim 30$--$50$~yr --- which means the
Dogger experience sits right at the predicted onset of decline. The
2024 reanalysis by Renaud \emph{et al.}\ [31] places the onset at
$\sim 50$--$70$~yr for typical Dogger spacings. Our analytical
estimate for Aspern of $\sim 62$~yr therefore falls within the
empirically observed band of low-enthalpy doublet lifetimes in
analogous reservoirs --- not a surprise, since the formula is
calibrated to those data.

\paragraph{Sensitivity table.} Thermal breakthrough time scales as
$t_{\mathrm{TB}} \propto L^{2}\,h / Q$ (with weak dependence on
$\phi$). The sensitivity is heavily dominated by spacing:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\hline
\textbf{Perturbation} & $t_{\mathrm{TB}}$ [yr] & \textbf{Comment} \\
\hline
Base case ($L=1500$~m, $h=100$~m, $Q=75$~kg/s) & 62  & --- \\
$L=1000$~m (tighter spacing)                    & 28  & half spacing, quarter lifetime \\
$L=2000$~m (wider spacing)                      & 110 & favoured for sustainability \\
$h=50$~m (thinner aquifer)                      & 31  & thin-bed reservoir \\
$h=200$~m (full Aderklaa interval)              & 124 & best-case thickness \\
$Q=150$~kg/s (higher production)                & 31  & doubles thermal output, halves life \\
$\phi=0.30$ (high porosity)                     & 66  & weak effect (higher $(\rho c)_{\mathrm{bulk}}$) \\
$\phi=0.10$ (low porosity)                      & 57  & weak effect (lower $(\rho c)_{\mathrm{bulk}}$) \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\textbf{Operational implication.} A 30-year plant lifetime
(Wien~Energie's stated horizon for first-wave doublets) sits
comfortably below the base-case $t_{\mathrm{TB}}\approx 62$~yr,
so the first generation of Vienna~Basin doublets are unlikely to
hit thermal breakthrough during their nominal economic life.
But: closer spacing or higher pumping rates would change this, and
this is exactly the design-point question Wien~Energie has not
publicly answered.

\paragraph{Multi-doublet thermal interference (Phase~3d).} For a
40-doublet deployment across the $\sim 1500$~km$^2$ Aderklaa
Conglomerate target area, we re-did the analysis with a proper
multi-doublet thermal-halo model (notebook
\texttt{notebooks/t7\_multidoublet.py}). The cooled-rock volume
around an injector is set by the heat-balance equation
\[
V_{\mathrm{cool}}(t) = Q\,t\,
\frac{(\rho c)_{\mathrm{w}}}{(\rho c)_{\mathrm{bulk}}}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
r_{\mathrm{halo}}(t) = \sqrt{\frac{V_{\mathrm{cool}}(t)}{\pi h}}.
\]
For the standard Aspern doublet
($Q = 0.075$~m$^3$/s, $h = 100$~m, $(\rho c)_{\mathrm{w}}/(\rho
c)_{\mathrm{bulk}} \approx 1.61$) this gives:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\hline
\textbf{$t$~[yr]} & \textbf{$r_{\mathrm{halo}}$~[m]} \\
\hline
1    & 110  \\
10   & 348  \\
20   & 493  \\
30   & 603  \\
50   & 779  \\
100  & 1101 \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\textbf{Critical separation for 30-yr operation.} Two halos
touch when $D = 2\,r_{\mathrm{halo}}(t_{\mathrm{int}})$, giving
$t_{\mathrm{int}} \propto D^{2}$. At
$D = 1$~km: $t_{\mathrm{int}} = 21$~yr (\emph{within plant
lifetime!}); at $D = 1.5$~km: 46~yr; at $D = 3$~km: 185~yr; at
$D = 6$~km: 742~yr. The critical separation for 30-yr operation
is $D_{\mathrm{crit}} = 2\,r_{\mathrm{halo}}(30) = 1.21$~km.

\textbf{40 doublets in 1500~km$^2$.} On a hexagonal grid the
nearest-neighbour distance is $d_{\mathrm{NN}} = 3.80$~km
($\sqrt{2A/(N\sqrt{3})}$); on a square grid 6.12~km. Either way
the safety factor over $D_{\mathrm{crit}}$ is $> 3\times$.
\emph{Direct thermal interference is not a first-order concern at
the 40-doublet basin scale for plant-lifetime operation.} The
geometric upper bound on deployment density at 30-yr
no-interference is $\sim 1190$~doublets in the same area --- a
factor of $\sim 30$ above any plausible utility target. Vienna
Basin geothermal is not space-constrained on these grounds.

\textbf{Cumulative cooled basin fraction.} For $N=40$ doublets
operated 30~yr, the total cooled volume is $\sim 4.6 \cdot
10^{9}$~m$^3$ = 3.0\% of the 150~km$^3$ deployment volume; at
100~yr, $\sim 10\%$. The Vienna Basin would still be far from
``thermally exhausted'' even on multi-century timescales at the
proposed deployment density.

\textbf{Pressure interference --- different physics, different
conclusion.} Hydraulic diffusivity ($\sim 1$~m$^2$/s) is
$\sim 10^{6}\times$ thermal, so pressure perturbations from each
doublet reach basin scale within hours to days. But the
\emph{doublet} pressure field is a dipole (producer + injector)
so its far-field falls as $\sim 1/D^{2}$ rather than the
$\sim \ln D$ of a single well. With $Q = 0.075$~m$^3$/s,
$\mu \approx 2.8 \cdot 10^{-4}$~Pa$\cdot$s, $k \approx
10^{-13}$~m$^2$, $h = 100$~m, $L = 1.5$~km, $D = 6$~km, the net
dipole pressure perturbation per neighbour is
$\Delta p \sim 0.02$~MPa; superposing six hex-grid neighbours
gives an order-of-magnitude cumulative of $\sim 0.1$~MPa --- at
the lower end of the empirical fault-triggering threshold
($\sim 0.01$--$0.1$~MPa) and well within the T2
single-doublet Coulomb-stress estimate ($\sim 0.2$--$2$~MPa)
that already governs the traffic-light proposal.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig_halo.pdf}
\caption{Left: single-doublet thermal halo radius $r_{\mathrm{halo}}(t)$
on log--log axes (Bodvarsson--Tsang heat balance applied to the

\textbf{Net conclusion for the Vienna Basin 40-doublet vision.}
At realistic spacings ($> 3$~km), thermal interference is
negligible; pressure interference is non-zero but
small-compared-to single-doublet stress effects. The T2
traffic-light radius of 10~km already covers the multi-doublet
case. \emph{No new operational constraints emerge from the
multi-doublet analysis at the proposed deployment density.}

\paragraph{Cooling-induced stress and the thread~2 link.} The
cooled volume above implies a thermoelastic stress redistribution
on the host rock. For free linear thermal contraction with
$\alpha = 10^{-5}$/K, $\Delta T = 60$~K, Young's modulus
$E = 30$~GPa, and Poisson's ratio $\nu = 0.25$, the
plane-strain contraction stress is
$\Delta\sigma \sim E\alpha\Delta T/(1-\nu)\approx 24$~MPa
\emph{within} the cooled volume. The Kivi \emph{et al.}\ (2022) [32]
modelling shows that this stress is transmitted ahead of the
cooled region and can perturb faults at kilometre-scale
distances at the 0.1--1~MPa level --- the order of the
``traffic-light'' threshold used in Bavarian Molasse Basin
practice. \emph{This is the quantitative link between long-term
geothermal operation and induced seismicity}: not direct
injection-pressure reactivation (thread~2 main physics), but
thermal-stress reactivation of distant faults over 30+ year
horizons. It is a slow phenomenon, not a startup phenomenon,
and is therefore under-monitored in current practice.

\paragraph{Falsification (what would change our conclusion).}
\begin{itemize}
\item The Aderklaa Conglomerate at Aspern turns out to be
\emph{thinner} than the 100~m assumed here (a 20--30~m net
reservoir cuts $t_{\mathrm{TB}}$ to 12--19~yr, within an
operational lifetime). Resolving this requires actual exploration
well data, which is at OMV.
\item Aspern's effective doublet spacing is dictated by the
Aderklaa-96 well location and surface land-use constraints
rather than by an optimisation, and ends up below 1~km.
\item Vienna Basin reservoir heterogeneity creates preferential
flow paths that short-circuit the assumed radial geometry
(realistic for a braided-river conglomerate); empirical breakthrough
could be much faster than the analytical estimate.
\item The Kivi \emph{et al.}\ thermo-elastic mechanism produces
$> 1$~MPa stress changes at VBTFS splay-fault depths within
30~yr, in which case thread~2's quantitative hazard assessment
needs updating.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Open questions for the retreat.}
\begin{itemize}
\item Analytical thermal-breakthrough estimate for an Aspern doublet with
Aderklaa Conglomerate properties (porosity $\sim 20$\,\%, thickness
$\sim 100$\,m, $L \sim 1.5$\,km) and the Aderklaa-96 reinjection rate
$\sim 40$~m$^3$/h. How does the predicted breakthrough time compare with
the Dogger observation that 50+ years have passed without measurable
decline?
\item Multi-doublet interference: at what doublet density does the
collective thermal field interaction become a first-order constraint on
the maximum sustainable extraction rate per km$^2$ of basin? A 2-D
heat-diffusion simulation with a realistic Vienna Basin geometry would
be illuminating.
\item Coupling to thread 2: estimate the cooling-induced
poroelastic Coulomb stress change at the depth of the VBTF splay faults
over 30~yr of operation. Is it large enough to matter for seismic hazard?
\item Re-pointing economics: at what predicted decline rate does
re-completing or sidetracking become cheaper than drilling a new doublet?
This is a small but well-defined LCOH problem.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Which thread to pick?}

A pragmatic ranking, given a retreat-plus-followup timescale:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Thread 5 (geothermal vs.\ heat pumps)} is now the
highest-value thread in policy terms --- Wien Energie is actively
deploying both technologies in parallel and the implicit mix
question is genuinely open. A clean optimisation answer would be
publishable and useful.
\item \textbf{Thread 4 (storage coupling)} remains the most tractable
single-deliverable thread and the best aligned with Wien Energie's
stated open questions.
\item \textbf{Thread 7 (reservoir depletion)} is well-defined, has a
clean analytical core (thermal breakthrough scaling) and a strong
empirical baseline (Dogger 40-yr data); also feeds directly into
threads 2, 4, and 6.
\item \textbf{Thread 2 (seismicity)} is the most physics-rich and the
most pedagogically valuable --- rate-and-state friction, pore-pressure
diffusion, fault mechanics all in one place --- but the deliverable
is harder to wrap up cleanly.
\item \textbf{Thread 3 (lithium)} has the highest novelty ceiling
because the Vienna Basin Li data gap is real. A focused literature
review plus an order-of-magnitude bound is achievable and
publication-relevant.
\item \textbf{Thread 6 (CO$_2$ working fluid)} is the most speculative
but has just been operationally validated by Eavor at Geretsried
(Dec 2025), so the timing for a follow-up academic comparison is
ideal --- with the caveat that the thermodynamics are heavy and a
single retreat may not be enough.
\item \textbf{Thread 1 (well repurposing)} is the most concrete
because Aderklaa-96 is a live experiment with public data. Good if
the group prefers a single-case-study format.
\end{itemize}

Natural pairings:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Reservoir hydraulics pair:} threads 2, 4, and 7 all
share heat-and-pressure-diffusion modelling in the same aquifer
geometry. They can share a single underlying numerical model and
divide labour by which physics is foregrounded.
\item \textbf{Legacy-well pair:} threads 1, 3, and 6 all use the
existing OMV well inventory as their starting point (the wells
themselves; the brine chemistry inside them; the candidates for
sCO$_2$ retrofit respectively).
\item \textbf{Systems pair:} threads 4 and 5 share the same demand
curve and would benefit from a single shared optimisation framework.
\end{itemize}
A retreat that splits into three sub-groups along these three pairings
is a sensible structure, with the proviso that the underlying
shared-data work (see ``What is still missing'' below) needs to be
done jointly first.
